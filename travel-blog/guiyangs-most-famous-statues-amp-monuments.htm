Guiyang’s Most Famous Statues & Monuments

  1. Home
  2. Travel Blog
  3. Blog Details

Guiyang, the capital of Guizhou province, is often celebrated as a "Forest City," a cool summer retreat nestled within China's lush karst landscape. Travelers flock here for the spicy cuisine, the minority cultures, and the breathtaking natural scenery. Yet, woven into the fabric of its hills and rivers is a different kind of landscape—a landscape of memory, aspiration, and identity cast in bronze and stone. The city's most famous statues and monuments are not mere decorations; they are silent narrators of Guiyang's soul, offering visitors a profound connection to its past, its struggles, and its vibrant, contemporary spirit. To explore them is to take a curated walk through the heart of Southwest China.

The Guardians of History and Culture

Before diving into the modern metropolis, one must first pay respects to the figures who shaped the region's intellectual and cultural foundations. These monuments serve as anchors, reminding a fast-developing city of its deep roots.

Wang Yangming and the School of Mind

Tucked away in the serene Xiufeng District, near the historic Jiaxiu Pavilion, stands a dignified statue of the Ming dynasty philosopher Wang Yangming. This is more than a tribute to a historical figure; it is a monument to an idea. Exiled to this then-remote frontier in the 16th century, Wang developed his revolutionary School of Mind, preaching the unity of knowledge and action. The statue, often seen with students and contemplative visitors nearby, symbolizes Guiyang’s unexpected role as a cradle of Neo-Confucian thought. For the culturally curious traveler, this site is a must-visit. It’s a quiet spot for reflection, often included in heritage walks, and a poignant reminder that profound enlightenment can bloom even in the most rugged of places. The surrounding area, with its traditional architecture and tea houses, allows you to linger in the atmosphere that once inspired a philosophical revolution.

The Legacy of Zhang Xiu: The Heroine of Guizhou

While not as ancient, the memory of Zhang Xiu, a legendary Miao ethnic heroine, is powerfully etched into the regional consciousness. Representations of her, often in vibrant murals or sculptures in cultural villages and museums around Guiyang, speak to the resilience and pride of the local minority communities. She represents the fierce spirit of resistance and autonomy. For tourists, encountering her image is an entry point into the rich tapestry of Guizhou’s ethnic diversity. It’s a direct link to the cultural performances, handicraft markets, and village tours that are major tourism draws. Her story underscores why a trip to Guiyang is incomplete without venturing into the surrounding communities—the monuments in the city point directly to the living culture in the countryside.

Symbols of a City's Identity

Some monuments transcend individual history to become the very emblem of the city itself. They appear on postcards, serve as default meeting points, and offer that essential photo opportunity that says, "I was in Guiyang."

Jiaxiu Pavilion: The Literary Heart

Although primarily an architectural marvel, the Jiaxiu Pavilion complex on the Fuyu Bridge is centered around a cultural mission immortalized in stone and wood. The pavilion itself, first built in 1598, was dedicated to nurturing scholarly achievement. While not a statue in the traditional sense, its presence is monumental. It functions as the number one iconic landmark. By day, tourists throng its halls and climb its pagoda for panoramic views of the Nanming River. By night, it becomes a dazzling spectacle of light, its reflection shimmering on the water—a prime focus for Guiyang’s booming night photography and evening cruise tours. The surrounding plaza is a hive of activity, with street food vendors, calligraphers, and performers, making it a perfect microcosm of Guiyang’s blend of history and daily life.

The Galloping Horse: A Metaphor for Progress

In the bustling business district, one might encounter a different kind of symbol: the dynamic, modernist statue of a galloping horse. This is a common motif in many Chinese cities, and in Guiyang, it carries specific weight. It represents the city’s dramatic leap from an isolated, mountainous region to a thriving tech and big data hub, often dubbed China’s "Big Data Valley." This statue isn’t about the past; it’s about the breathtaking speed of the present and future. For the business traveler or the tourist curious about contemporary China, this monument speaks volumes. It’s often located near gleaming new developments, high-speed rail links, and innovation centers, highlighting a key tourism peripheral hotspot: the story of China’s rapid modernization is vividly on display here, contrasting beautifully with the ancient hills.

Monuments of Memory and Harmony

Guiang’s landscape also holds spaces for collective memory and reflection on broader human themes. These sites attract visitors seeking a deeper, more somber, or more universal connection.

A common feature in many Chinese cities, including Guiyang, are public monuments and sculptures dedicated to themes of peace, anti-war, and sometimes international friendship. These can take abstract forms—doves, broken chains, harmonious figures—and serve as community spaces for reflection. For the international traveler, they offer a point of shared understanding and a reminder of universal values. They are often found in public parks, like Qianling Park or the People’s Square, which are themselves major tourism hotspots for those looking to see locals engaged in tai chi, dance, or leisurely strolls. Visiting these spaces provides a slice of daily life and a moment of quiet amidst the urban energy.

The Bronze Drum Sculpture: Echoes of Ancient Cultures

Scattered throughout the city, particularly near cultural centers and museums, you may find sculptures inspired by the ancient Bronze Drum, a sacred object of the Baiyue peoples and later the Zhuang and other ethnic groups of the southwest. These monumental drum sculptures are instantly recognizable. They are a direct artistic bridge to the incredible archaeological finds displayed at the Guizhou Provincial Museum—a top-tier attraction for any serious tourist. They symbolize the ancient, sophisticated civilizations that thrived in this region long before it was called Guiyang. Photography tours and cultural itineraries often use these sculptures as striking visual motifs, connecting the modern city plaza to the mysterious, artifact-rich past explored in museum halls.

The Living Monument: Qiangshengmen and the City Wall

Finally, some monuments are not single statues but integrated structures. The restored Qiangshengmen (South Gate) and fragments of the old city wall stand as a powerful physical testament to Guiyang’s historical footprint. Walking along this section, near the bustling Zhonghua Road shopping district, is a journey through layers of time. It’s a favorite spot for history buffs and photographers, especially during the golden hour when the ancient stones glow against the modern skyline. This site is central to the popular "Old Guiyang" walking tours, which expertly weave tales of the past with stops at nearby famous snack streets. It’s a perfect example of how monument tourism seamlessly blends with culinary tourism—after contemplating centuries of history, you can immediately sample local specialties like Changwang Noodles or Siwawa, making the experience fully immersive.

From the philosopher’s quiet gaze to the horse’s dynamic leap, from the ethnic heroine’s defiance to the pavilion’s luminous grace, Guiyang’s statues and monuments form an essential itinerary. They are the waypoints on a journey that moves beyond the stunning Huangguoshu Waterfall or the enchanting Qianling Mountain Park. They tell the story of a place that has long been a crossroads of ideas, a fortress of culture, and now, a sprinting pioneer of the future. To seek them out is to engage in a dialogue with the city itself, discovering that its true character is carved not just into the karst, but into the bronze and stone that stand watch over its ever-evolving story.
