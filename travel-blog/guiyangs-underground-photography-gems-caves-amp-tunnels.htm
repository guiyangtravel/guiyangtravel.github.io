Guiyang’s Underground Photography Gems: Caves & Tunnels

  1. Home
  2. Travel Blog
  3. Blog Details

The typical travel brochure for Guiyang, the capital of Guizhou province, will dazzle you with images of lush, rolling green hills, the sparkling waters of the Qianling Park lake, and the vibrant, colorful cultures of the Miao and Gejia peoples. These are the postcard-perfect scenes, the ones bathed in golden hour light. But to truly understand the soul of this city, to capture its essence through a lens, you must be willing to descend. Beneath the verdant canopy and the bustling urban sprawl lies another world—a subterranean realm of breathtaking scale, haunting beauty, and profound silence. This is the domain of the karst landscape, a paradise for photographers seeking the unique, the dramatic, and the profoundly atmospheric.

For the adventurous photographer, Guiyang’s underground isn't just a subject; it's a co-creator. It challenges your technical skills, rewards your patience, and transforms your perspective on light, composition, and time itself.

Qianlong Cave: The Grand Cathedral of Light and Shadow

No exploration of Guiyang’s underground photography scene is complete without a pilgrimage to Qianlong Cave (Qianlong Dong). Located just outside the city center, this isn't merely a cave; it's a colossal natural cathedral. The name itself evokes imperial grandeur, and the space does not disappoint. The moment you step inside, your sense of scale is recalibrated. The ceiling soars to impossible heights, and the main chamber is vast enough to swallow entire buildings.

Chasing the Divine Beam

The primary photographic draw of Qianlong Cave is its manipulation of light. Unlike the absolute darkness of many caves, Qianlong has openings in its ceiling that act as natural spotlights. On a clear day, particularly around midday, sharp, powerful beams of sunlight pierce the gloom, illuminating vast swathes of the cave floor and the intricate rock formations within. These are the "Jesus rays" or "God beams" that every landscape photographer dreams of capturing.

To photograph this phenomenon successfully, you need to be strategic. A sturdy tripod is non-negotiable. You’ll be working with long exposures to capture the details in the shadows while managing the intense brightness of the light shafts. Use a wide-angle lens (16-35mm on a full-frame sensor is ideal) to encompass the sheer scale of the chamber and the dramatic journey of the light from ceiling to floor. A remote shutter release will prevent any camera shake during those crucial long exposures. The interplay between the warm, ethereal light and the cool, dark rock creates a chiaroscuro effect worthy of a Caravaggio painting.

Composing the Grand and the Minute

While the light beams are the star of the show, don't ignore the cave’s other features. Use the ancient stalactites and stalagmites in the foreground to create a sense of depth, leading the viewer’s eye into the frame towards the illuminated areas. Look for patterns in the flowstone, the texture of the walls, and the reflective surfaces of small pools of water. Sometimes, a more intimate composition focusing on a single, beautifully lit formation can be as powerful as the grand vista. The human element can also add scale and narrative; a solitary figure silhouetted against a light beam creates a poignant and powerful image.

The Abandoned 7618 Tunnel: A Portal to a Frozen Era

If Qianlong Cave represents nature's sublime artistry, the 7618 Tunnel represents a different kind of history—one of human industry and subsequent abandonment. This is where urban exploration (urbex) photography truly comes alive. The 7618 Tunnel complex is a relic of the Third Front Movement (三线建设) era, a vast network of tunnels and factories built in the 1960s and 70s for national defense, now left to the elements.

Mastering the Atmosphere of Decay

Photographing the 7618 Tunnel is less about majestic beauty and more about capturing a mood. The air is thick with the scent of damp concrete and history. Peeling propaganda posters, rusted machinery, and crumbling walls are your subjects. Your goal is to tell a story of time standing still.

Your essential gear here includes a good tripod and fast prime lenses. The light is low and often unpredictable. A 24mm or 35mm lens is perfect for capturing environmental scenes, while a 50mm lens can help you isolate compelling details. Don't be afraid to push your ISO, but be mindful of noise. The gritty texture of the location can often forgive a bit of digital grain, which can even enhance the aesthetic.

Telling Stories with Relics

Look for the narratives embedded in the decay. A single, rusted worker’s helmet sitting on a control panel. Faded Chinese characters painted on a wall, their message now a ghost. The perfect geometry of the receding tunnel arches, creating a mesmerizing leading line into darkness. Use your flashlight or a off-camera flash to creatively paint with light, selectively illuminating a key subject while leaving the surrounding mystery intact. Black and white processing is highly effective here, emphasizing texture, contrast, and the timeless, melancholic feel of the place.

Beyond the Classics: Other Subterranean Highlights

Guiyang’s karst foundation means the opportunities are nearly endless. While Qianlong Cave and the 7618 Tunnel are the headliners, the truly intrepid photographer can find countless other gems.

Zhenshan Ethnographic Village Cave

This spot offers a unique blend of natural wonder and cultural heritage. Here, a Miao village is built directly into the mouth of a massive cave, with traditional wooden houses nestled under the vast limestone overhang. The photographic opportunities are incredible. You can capture the contrast between the organic curves of the rock and the geometric lines of the architecture. Early morning is magical, as the mist rises from the ground and smoke from breakfast fires curls up towards the cave ceiling, backlit by the soft morning light. It’s a scene that feels both ancient and alive.

Undiscovered Karst Sinkholes and Rift Caves

Venturing into the countryside surrounding Guiyang will reveal countless smaller caves, sinkholes (tiankeng), and fissures. These require more effort and often a local guide, but the rewards are unparalleled. Rappelling into a sinkhole to photograph a "secret garden" at the bottom, where rare flora thrives in the humid, protected environment, is an experience straight out of an adventure film. These locations demand a robust kit, including ultra-wide-angle lenses to capture the confined spaces and macro lenses for the unique fungi and insects that call these places home.

The Photographer's Toolkit for the Underworld

Venturing underground requires more than just creative vision; it requires practical preparation.

  • The Unshakable Tripod: This is your most important piece of equipment. The darkness demands long exposures, from several seconds to several minutes.
  • Lens Arsenal: A wide-angle lens (14-24mm or 16-35mm) is essential for vast chambers. A fast prime (e.g., f/1.8 or f/1.4) in the 24mm to 50mm range is invaluable for handheld shots in dimly lit tunnels. A macro lens can reveal a hidden world of details.
  • Lighting is Everything: A powerful, reliable headlamp is for your safety and navigation. But for photography, bring off-camera lighting. A good flashlight can be used for light painting. Consider a small, portable LED panel for filling in shadows.
  • Protection and Comfort: The underground environment is damp, dirty, and often slippery. Wear sturdy, waterproof hiking boots with good grip. Pack your camera gear in a protective bag and have plenty of lens cloths to combat condensation. A lightweight rain jacket is also advisable.
  • Respect and Responsibility: Many of these locations, especially the abandoned tunnels, are fragile. The motto "take only pictures, leave only footprints" has never been more critical. Be mindful of your impact, do not disturb artifacts, and always prioritize your safety. For wild caves, never go alone and always inform someone of your plans.

The call of the underground is a powerful one for a photographer. It’s a journey away from the familiar, saturated world above and into a realm of subtlety, mystery, and raw, geological power. In Guiyang, the earth itself opens up, offering not just a photoshoot, but a profound experience. It’s in the silent, cool darkness of a cave or the echoing emptiness of a forgotten tunnel that you’ll find some of the most compelling and unforgettable images, frames that hold not just light, but the very weight of time and stone.
