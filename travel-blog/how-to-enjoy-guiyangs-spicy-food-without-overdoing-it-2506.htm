How to Enjoy Guiyang’s Spicy Food Without Overdoing It

  1. Home
  2. Travel Blog
  3. Blog Details

Guiyang, the capital of Guizhou Province, is a paradise for food lovers—especially those who crave bold, spicy flavors. From the iconic sour and spicy fish hot pot to the addictive siwawa (a fresh spring roll packed with herbs and chili), the city’s cuisine is a fiery celebration of local ingredients and culinary creativity. However, for travelers unaccustomed to such intense heat, diving headfirst into Guiyang’s spice scene can be overwhelming. Here’s how to savor every bite without regretting it later.

Understanding Guiyang’s Spice Profile

Before jumping into the culinary deep end, it helps to know what makes Guiyang’s spice unique. Unlike Sichuan’s mala (numbing spice) or Hunan’s dry heat, Guizhou’s spice is often balanced with sour, herbal, and fermented notes.

Key Ingredients to Know

  • Chili Peppers: Guizhou loves its la jiao, but the heat varies. Zunyi chili is smoky, while Huaxi chili is brighter and fruitier.
  • Suan Tang (Sour Soup): A tangy, fermented base made with tomatoes or rice broth, often tempering the spice.
  • Zhe’ergen: A pungent local herb that adds a sharp, medicinal kick to dishes like liangfen (cold mung bean jelly).

Start Slow: Build Your Spice Tolerance

If you’re new to Guiyang’s heat, don’t attempt a la zi ji (chili chicken) challenge on day one. Ease in with these milder-but-flavorful dishes:

Beginner-Friendly Picks

  1. Yangrou Fen (Lamb Rice Noodles): A hearty bowl with a clear, peppery broth—spicy but not overwhelming.
  2. Qianxi Tofu: Fried tofu stuffed with minced meat and herbs, served with a mild dipping sauce.
  3. Baba Cake: A sticky rice snack with sweet or savory fillings; opt for the non-spicy versions.

Pro tip: Ask for wei la ("mild spice") or bu yao tai la ("not too spicy") when ordering.

Hydrate Like a Local

Spicy food demands smart hydration. Guiyang locals swear by these remedies:

Must-Try Drinks

  • Sour Plum Juice (Suan Mei Tang): A sweet-tart cooler that neutralizes heat.
  • Mijiu (Fermented Rice Wine): A light, slightly sweet alcohol believed to aid digestion.
  • Herbal Tea (Liang Cha): Look for stalls selling wanglaoji or homemade blends with chrysanthemum or mint.

Avoid icy water—it can shock your system. Room-temperature or warm drinks work best.

The Art of Pairing

Balance is key. Combine fiery dishes with cooling sides:

Smart Combos

  • Spicy Hot Pot + Fresh Veggies: Dip tong hao (crown daisy) or dou miao (pea shoots) in broth to offset the burn.
  • Grilled Fish with Chili + Cold Cucumber Salad: The crunch and coolness provide relief.
  • Zha Jiang Mian (Noodles with Spicy Sauce) + Soy Milk: A classic breakfast duo that soothes the palate.

Know When to Stop

Guiyang’s spice creeps up on you. Watch for these signs you’ve hit your limit:

  • Sweating profusely (beyond the usual "spicy glow").
  • Numb tongue or lips: A sign you’ve overdone the hua jiao (Sichuan pepper).
  • Stomach discomfort: Time for mantou (plain steamed buns) to absorb excess spice.

Spice-Free Breaks

Give your taste buds a rest with these non-spicy gems:

Mild Delights

  • Guiyang-style Breakfast Porridge: Rice congee with pickles or preserved eggs.
  • Sweet Sticky Rice Balls (Tangyuan): Filled with sesame or red bean paste.
  • Grilled Rice Cakes (Nian Gao): Chewy and slightly sweet, often sold at night markets.

Embrace the Aftermath

Even with precautions, you might overindulge. Here’s how locals recover:

  • Yogurt or Milk: Dairy helps neutralize capsaicin. Try suan nai (local yogurt).
  • Bananas or Honey: Natural remedies for stomach irritation.
  • A Walk Along Nanming River: Gentle movement aids digestion—plus, the scenery distracts from the burn.

Guiyang’s spice isn’t just heat—it’s a cultural experience. By pacing yourself and pairing wisely, you’ll leave with a newfound appreciation for Guizhou’s fiery soul (and maybe a higher spice tolerance).
