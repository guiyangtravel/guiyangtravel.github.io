Guiyang’s Best Dishes for Summer Heat Relief

  1. Home
  2. Travel Blog
  3. Blog Details

The summer sun beats down on the rolling, green hills of Guizhou, but in the provincial capital of Guiyang, a unique kind of coolness awaits. It’s not just the altitude that provides respite; it’s a culinary philosophy woven into the very fabric of the city’s food scene. Guiyang doesn’t simply endure the heat; it eats its way through it. For the traveler, exploring this city becomes a delicious mission of heat relief, where every meal is an opportunity to cool the body, awaken the senses, and dive into a food culture designed for subtropical living. This is a journey where spice meets refreshment, and sour notes dance with herbal remedies, all in pursuit of the perfect, cooling bite.

The Philosophy of "Qing Re Jie Shu": Eating for Coolness

To understand Guiyang’s summer table, you must grasp the concept of "Qing Re Jie Shu." Roughly translating to "clearing heat and relieving summer dryness," it’s a principle rooted in traditional Chinese dietary therapy. The idea is that certain ingredients possess cooling properties that balance the body’s internal heat, which is exacerbated by hot, humid weather. Guiyang’s cuisine masterfully applies this. Instead of heavy, oily dishes, you’ll find a predominance of sour and spicy flavors, fresh herbs, chilled broths, and ingredients like bitter melon, cucumber, and mint. The food is designed to make you sweat briefly (to expel heat) and then leave you feeling remarkably refreshed and cooled from the inside out. It’s functional eating at its most delicious, turning a meal into a wellness ritual for the weary, heat-soaked traveler.

The Unbeatable Icons: Guiyang’s Signature Cool-Down Dishes

These are the non-negotiable, must-try classics that define a Guiyang summer. No food tour is complete without them.

Sour Soup Fish (Suān Tāng Yú): The King of Cooling Broths

This is Guiyang’s most famous ambassador for a reason. Imagine a vibrant, crimson broth, shimmering with a complex aroma of fermented tomatoes, wild mountain tomatoes (shān cài), and a careful blend of herbs. The sourness is bright, tangy, and deeply appetizing, not vinegary. Within this simmering pot lies tender fish fillets, usually carp or catfish. The magic happens when you take a spoonful of the hot soup. It initially warms you, but the sourness immediately triggers a cooling, thirst-quenching response. Served with a dipping sauce of toasted chili and cilantro, it’s a symphony of flavors that energizes and cools simultaneously. For the traveler, sharing a pot of Sour Soup Fish in a bustling restaurant is a rite of passage and the most effective internal air-conditioning you’ll find.

Silk娃娃 (Sī Wá Wá): The DIY Refreshing Salad

Perhaps the most interactive and visually stunning summer dish is Sī Wá Wá, or "Silk Doll." It’s a build-your-own salad wrap centered around a stack of translucent, paper-thin rice sheets. You’re presented with a platter of colorful, finely julienned ingredients: crisp cucumber, crunchy bean sprouts, shredded kelp, pickled radish, fresh cilantro, and beansprouts. The centerpiece is a uniquely Guiyang sauce—a thick, savory paste made from fermented soybeans. You take a rice sheet, layer on your chosen veggies, add a dab of the pungent, umami-rich sauce, and sometimes a drizzle of chili oil, then wrap it into a neat bundle. The result is an explosion of textures and temperatures: cool, crisp vegetables against the warm, fermented depth of the sauce. It’s light, hydrating, fun to eat, and perfectly embodies the local wisdom of eating fresh, raw veggies to combat the heat.

Mǐ Dòu Fu & Liáng Fěn: The Street Food Chill

When you’re walking through the vibrant chaos of Qingyan Ancient Town or the Erqi Road snack street, these are your go-to portable coolants. Mǐ Dòu fu (Rice Tofu) is a grayish, jelly-like block made from rice and plant ash. It’s served cold, sliced into cubes, and drenched in a sauce of soy vinegar, chili oil, pickled radish, and peanuts. Its slippery, smooth texture and bracing, sour-spicy sauce are instantly reviving. Similarly, Liáng fěn (Chilled Rice Noodles) are slippery noodles served ice-cold in a vinegary, garlicky, and spicy broth, often topped with crispy soybeans. Slurping a bowl under a shaded alleyway is a quintessential Guiyang summer moment.

Beyond the Plate: Cooling Sips and Sweet Escapes

The heat relief continues in your glass and dessert bowl. Guiyang’s beverage and dessert game is strong and purpose-built.

Mǎ Lēng Shuǐ: The Herbal Elixir

This dark, mysterious-looking drink is a local legend. Brewed from a special local herb, it has a subtly sweet, earthy, and slightly medicinal flavor. It’s served chilled and is believed to have exceptional heat-clearing and detoxifying properties. You’ll see locals sipping it from large dispensers at street stalls. For the adventurous traveler, it’s a must-try—a genuine taste of local folk medicine that’s both curiously refreshing and culturally immersive.

Ice Jelly (Bīng Fěn) & Sweet Soup (Tián Jiǔ)

For a pure, sweet cool-down, nothing beats a bowl of Bīng fěn. This clear, crystal-like jelly made from plant seeds is shaved into a bowl, topped with honey, fruit syrup, raisins, and nuts, and served with ice. It’s like eating sweet, flavored snow. Tián jiǔ, on the other hand, is a fermented sweet rice soup, often served chilled. It’s lightly alcoholic, sweet, and tangy, with a creamy texture from the broken rice grains. It’s a comforting, cooling treat that feels both ancient and utterly satisfying.

The Traveler’s Itinerary: A Heat-Relief Food Crawl

To experience this culinary cool-down, structure your exploration around these hotspots. Start your day at Qianling Park. After walking by the macaques, find a vendor for a quick Mǎ Lēng Shuǐ. For lunch, head to the Dashizi area, where old-school restaurants serve monumental pots of Sour Soup Fish. In the afternoon, get lost in the flagstone streets of Qingyan Ancient Town. Here, snack continuously: grab a Sī Wá Wá to assemble yourself, a bowl of Liáng fěn from a stall, and sample various pickles and preserved meats (which, paradoxically, pair perfectly with cooling dishes). As evening falls, dive into the Erqi Road night market. The sensory overload is part of the fun. Follow the steam and sizzle to try Zhà Zhà Guǒ (a fried potato snack), grilled fish, and finish with a colorful bowl of Bīng fěn. For a more curated experience, a food tour led by a local guide can unlock hidden alleyway gems and explain the stories behind the flavors.

Remember, in Guiyang, the climate isn’t an obstacle to dining; it’s the inspiration. Each sour sip, each crunchy vegetable, each chilled noodle is a thoughtful response to the environment. It’s a city that invites you to eat intelligently, to find vitality in freshness, and to discover that the most memorable way to beat the heat is to savor it, one incredibly cooling dish at a time. So pack an appetite, leave your heavy creams and sauces behind, and get ready for a gastronomic adventure that will leave you feeling refreshed, energized, and deeply connected to Guiyang’s ingenious culinary soul.
