Best Places to Visit in Guiyang for First-Timers

  1. Home
  2. Travel Blog
  3. Blog Details

Guiyang, the capital of Guizhou Province, is a hidden gem in Southwest China. Known for its lush landscapes, vibrant ethnic culture, and mouthwatering cuisine, this city offers a unique blend of modernity and tradition. If you're visiting for the first time, here are the must-see spots that will make your trip unforgettable.

Top Attractions in Guiyang

1. Qianling Mountain Park

One of the city’s most iconic green spaces, Qianling Mountain Park is a perfect escape from urban hustle. The park is home to wild macaques, serene temples, and hiking trails that lead to breathtaking viewpoints.

  • Highlights:
    • Hongfu Temple: A centuries-old Buddhist temple nestled in the mountains.
    • Macaque Sightings: Friendly (but mischievous) monkeys roam freely.
    • Panoramic Views: Climb to the top for a stunning view of Guiyang.

2. Jiaxiu Pavilion

A symbol of Guiyang, Jiaxiu Pavilion stands elegantly over the Nanming River. This Ming Dynasty-era structure is especially magical at night when illuminated.

  • Why Visit?
    • Historical Significance: Built in 1598, it’s a masterpiece of ancient Chinese architecture.
    • Night Views: The reflection on the river creates a postcard-perfect scene.
    • Nearby Food Street: Sample local snacks like Changwang Noodles after your visit.

3. Qingyan Ancient Town

Step back in time at Qingyan Ancient Town, a well-preserved 600-year-old fortress town. Cobblestone streets, Ming-Qing architecture, and local delicacies make it a must-visit.

  • Don’t Miss:
    • Ancient City Walls: Walk along the historic fortifications.
    • Local Snacks: Try Qingyan Tofu and Rose Sugar.
    • Temple of the God of War: A quiet spot with intricate carvings.

Cultural & Unique Experiences

4. Guizhou Provincial Museum

To understand Guiyang’s rich ethnic diversity, head to the Guizhou Provincial Museum. Exhibits showcase Miao, Dong, and Buyi cultures through artifacts, textiles, and folk art.

  • Key Exhibits:
    • Silver Jewelry: Elaborate Miao headdresses and necklaces.
    • Traditional Costumes: Vibrant embroidery and indigo-dyed fabrics.
    • Interactive Displays: Learn about minority festivals like Sister’s Meal Festival.

5. Huaxi Wetland Park

A tranquil retreat, Huaxi Wetland Park features lakes, lotus ponds, and walking paths. It’s ideal for a leisurely afternoon or a bike ride.

  • Activities:
    • Boat Rides: Glide across the calm waters.
    • Photography: Capture the park’s seasonal beauty (especially in autumn).
    • Picnics: Relax by the water with local snacks.

Foodie Hotspots

6. Siwai Street (小吃街 - Xiaochi Jie)

No trip to Guiyang is complete without indulging in its street food. Siwai Street is a paradise for food lovers, offering bold flavors and unique dishes.

  • Must-Try Dishes:
    • Sour Soup Fish (酸汤鱼): A tangy, spicy local favorite.
    • Changwang Mian (肠旺面): Noodles with pork blood and intestines.
    • Zhu’er Gen (竹儿根): A refreshing fern-root salad.

7. Nanjing Road Food Market

For a more local experience, visit Nanjing Road in the evening. Vendors serve everything from grilled skewers to stinky tofu.

  • Pro Tip:
    • Go with an empty stomach—the variety is overwhelming!
    • Pair your meal with Moutai Beer or local rice wine.

Day Trips from Guiyang

8. Huangguoshu Waterfall

About 2 hours from Guiyang, Huangguoshu is China’s largest waterfall. The thunderous cascade and surrounding karst scenery are awe-inspiring.

  • Best Time to Visit:
    • Summer (June-August) for the fullest water flow.
    • Avoid holidays to escape crowds.

9. Kaili & Xijiang Miao Village

Dive deeper into ethnic culture with a trip to Xijiang, the world’s largest Miao village. Traditional wooden houses, silver craftsmanship, and lively dances await.

  • Cultural Highlights:
    • Lusheng Performances: Bamboo flute music and dance.
    • Night View: Thousands of lights illuminate the village.

Practical Tips for First-Timers

  • Weather: Guiyang is called the "City of Eternal Spring" for its mild climate. Pack layers—rain is common.
  • Transport: The metro system is expanding, but taxis and DiDi (China’s Uber) are convenient.
  • Language: English isn’t widely spoken, so have a translation app ready.

Guiyang is a city of contrasts—where ancient traditions meet modern energy. Whether you’re hiking through misty mountains, savoring fiery cuisine, or exploring ethnic villages, every moment here is an adventure.
