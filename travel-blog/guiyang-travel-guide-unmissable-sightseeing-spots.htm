Guiyang Travel Guide: Unmissable Sightseeing Spots

  1. Home
  2. Travel Blog
  3. Blog Details

Nestled in the rolling hills of Southwest China, Guiyang, the capital of Guizhou province, is a city that often flies under the radar for international travelers. Yet, those who venture here discover a destination of breathtaking natural beauty, a profoundly rich tapestry of ethnic cultures, and a culinary scene that is as fiery as it is flavorful. This is not a city of overwhelming imperial history or futuristic skylines; instead, Guiyang offers an authentic, grounded experience. It’s a gateway to some of China's most spectacular landscapes and a vibrant urban center where the pace of life feels refreshingly human. The air, famously fresh and cool, has earned it the moniker "The Forest City," providing a perpetual spring-like escape from the scorching summers of other Chinese metropolises. Prepare to be captivated by the symphony of bubbling hot pots, the sight of mist-shrouded karst peaks, and the warm hospitality of its people.

The Heart of the City: Urban Explorations

While many use Guiyang as a launchpad for rural adventures, the city itself holds a unique charm, a blend of modern development and enduring local character.

Jiaxiu Tower: The Iconic Landmark

No trip to Guiyang is complete without a visit to the majestic Jiaxiu Tower. Standing proudly on Fuyu Bridge over the Nanming River, this three-story, 20-meter tall wooden pagoda is the undeniable symbol of the city. Its name translates to "First Scholar's Tower," reflecting its historical connection to scholars who topped the imperial examinations. Climbing to the top offers a panoramic view of the modern city rising around this ancient core, especially enchanting at night when the tower is brilliantly illuminated, its reflection shimmering in the water below. The surrounding pavilions and gardens provide a peaceful respite from the urban buzz, a place where locals practice tai chi and elders gather for games of chess.

Qianling Mountain Park: A Green Lung

For a dose of nature without leaving the city, head to Qianling Mountain Park. This vast park is a local favorite for hiking, temple visits, and, most famously, interacting with the troops of macaque monkeys that call the hills home. A walk up the mountain paths leads you through lush forests to Hongfu Temple, a significant Zen Buddhist monastery. The atmosphere is one of serene spirituality, with the scent of incense hanging in the air. The real fun, however, begins with the monkeys. They are playful and inquisitive, but remember to secure your belongings and avoid direct eye contact or feeding them. The park also features the historic Kanzhu Cave and a serene lake where you can rent paddle boats.

Exploring the Culinary Heart: Er'ershi & Snack Streets

Guiyang’s food scene is an adventure in itself. The city center, particularly around the area known as "Er'ershi," is a labyrinth of food stalls and local eateries. As dusk falls, the city's snack streets come alive with a carnival of aromas and sounds. Here, you must be brave and dive in. Try Siwawa, a delicate spring roll wrapper filled with various fresh vegetables and a spicy dipping sauce. Sample Changwang Mian, a hearty noodle dish with a rich, blood-pudding-based broth. And for the truly adventurous, there’s Zhe'ergen, a root with a unique, pungent flavor that locals adore. It’s a culinary culture built on sour and spicy notes, guaranteed to wake up your taste buds.

Beyond the City: Day Trips to Natural Wonders

The true magic of the Guiyang region lies just beyond its borders, where some of China's most surreal and awe-inspiring landscapes await.

Qingyan Ancient Town: A Step Back in Time

Located just an hour's drive from downtown Guiyang, Qingyan Ancient Town is a beautifully preserved Ming and Qing dynasty town. Its stone-paved streets, ancient city walls, and intricate archways transport you to a bygone era. Unlike some overly commercialized ancient towns, Qingyan retains a sense of authenticity. You can wander through courtyards, visit temples, and watch local artisans at work. It's also a fantastic place for a food crawl. Don't miss the town's specialties: Qingyan Tofu, which comes in various forms from braised to fermented, and the rose candy, a sweet local delicacy. Climbing the old town walls provides a fantastic view of the tiled roofs and the surrounding countryside.

Huangguoshu Waterfall: China's Liquid Grandeur

This is the crown jewel of Guizhou’s tourism and for a good reason. Huangguoshu Waterfall is not just a waterfall; it is a colossal, thundering spectacle. As one of the largest waterfalls in Asia, it plunges 77.8 meters in a curtain of water that is 101 meters wide. The experience is multi-sensory—you hear its roar long before you see it, and the fine mist kisses your skin as you approach. The park allows you to experience the waterfall from multiple angles: from the front, from the side, and most impressively, from behind through the Water-Curtain Cave, a natural tunnel that runs directly behind the cascading water. Standing there, looking out through the roaring curtain, is a truly humbling and unforgettable experience.

Tianlong Tunpu: The Forgotten Han Enclave

For a cultural deep-dive, visit the fascinating community of Tianlong Tunpu. The residents here are descendants of Han Chinese soldiers sent to garrison this area during the Ming Dynasty. Isolated for centuries, they have preserved customs and a way of life that have long disappeared elsewhere in China. The most striking feature is the local women's attire, which is a traditional Han style from the Ming era. They also practice a unique form of ancient Han Dixi opera, performed with wooden masks. Walking through the stone villages and fortresses, you are witnessing a living museum, a slice of 600-year-old history frozen in time.

Venturing Deeper: The Heartland of Minority Culture

To truly understand Guizhou, one must journey into the prefectures where the ethnic Miao and Dong peoples have built their lives and cultures for millennia.

Kaili and the Miao Villages

Using Guiyang as a base, a trip to the city of Kaili opens the door to the world of the Miao people. The surrounding countryside is dotted with hundreds of traditional villages. Xijiang Thousand Household Miao Village is the largest Miao settlement in the world, a breathtaking sight with thousands of wooden stilted houses cascading down a mountain valley. While it can be touristy, its scale and the evening song and dance performance are spectacular. For a more intimate experience, seek out villages like Langde, where you might be welcomed with a "blocking the way" ceremony involving rice wine and traditional songs. The intricate silver headdresses and jewelry worn by the Miao women during festivals are some of the most magnificent in the world.

Liping and the Dong Culture

Venturing further east, the culture shifts to that of the Dong people, master architects known for their incredible wooden structures without using a single nail. The Zhaoxing Dong Village is a masterpiece, featuring five magnificent drum towers, each representing a different clan. The wind and rain bridges, which serve as functional bridges, meeting places, and spiritual shelters, are another architectural marvel. The best way to experience this is to stay overnight in a guesthouse, listen to the ancient Dong polyphonic songs under the stars, and wake up to the mist rising from the river that winds through the village.

The Practical Traveler's Guide

When to Visit

The best time to visit Guiyang is from March to October. Spring (March-May) brings pleasant temperatures and blooming flowers. Summer (June-August) is comfortably cool compared to the rest of China, making it a popular summer retreat. Autumn (September-October) is clear and mild. Avoid the week-long National Day holiday in early October, as domestic travel peaks during this time.

Getting Around

Guiyang’s modern metro system makes navigating the city a breeze. For attractions outside the city, the high-speed rail network is efficient and comfortable, connecting Guiyang to places like Kaili and beyond. For maximum flexibility, especially for reaching remote villages, hiring a private car with a driver for a day or two is a highly recommended and relatively affordable option.

A Taste of Guiyang: Must-Try Dishes

Beyond the snacks, sit down for a proper Guiyang meal. * Sour Soup Fish (Suantang Yu): The city's signature dish. A fermented tomato and chili-based broth, tangy, spicy, and incredibly flavorful, with a whole fresh fish simmered within. * Guiyang-style Hot Pot: Distinct from Sichuan or Chongqing hot pot, it often features a sour soup base or an incredibly spicy Maocai (a dry-ish hot pot where ingredients are pre-cooked). * Rice Noodles (Guizhou Fen): A breakfast staple. The most famous is Guizhou Beef Rice Noodles, served in a rich, herbal broth and customized with a dozen different condiments at your table.

The rhythm of life here is different. It’s in the slow sipping of tea by the river, the echoing songs from a Dong drum tower, and the shared laughter over a bubbling pot of sour soup. Guiyang and its surroundings don’t just show you scenery; they invite you into a way of life. It’s an unpolished gem, waiting for those who seek paths less traveled.
